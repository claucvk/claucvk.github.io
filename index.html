Design Process

The User Centered Design approach guides my practice. It's mean that I take the user's needs into account at every stage of the product lifecycle: User Research, Design, Testing, and Implementation. In the literature, the names, the classifications or the number of this stages could change. Nevertheless, they share similar meanings.

This is an iterative design process which means that the product is tested and evaluated repeatedly at different stages of design to eliminate flaws before the product is designed and launched. The tools used and the emphasize given to particular aspects of each stage depend on the characteristics of the product, the user's needs, and the organizational's goals.

1) User Research

The goal is to inform the design from the perspective of the front-end user. In this stage, we gather and analyze data from users. We answer questions such as: What are their needs and goals? How they use the product? What are their expectations? What are the pain points? to understand the current state of the interactions between the users and the product.

3) Testing

In order to explore and experiment the design ideas, we build prototypes and test them before getting started on the implementation phase. We show these prototypes to stakeholders (peers, client, users) to get their feedback, eliminate problems or user difficulties that were unforeseen in the design phase as well as check functionality and usability before any money is spent on full-blown development.

2) Design

This stage consists in creating a future state of the product. We translate the user's data into product's functionalities. In others words, we generate design ideas that may help solve the user problems identified in the research phase.

4) Implementation

This is the build phase where we take all the information and feedback gathered from prototyping and we begin to build the product with design layouts and build upon our already established front-end coding. Thus, resulting in your final iteration.
